Suncor Energy Inc. (May 2022 - Aug 2023)

I was at Suncor's Syncrude Aurora North site for 16 months, working in oil sands operations as a Mining Engineer Co-op. I was mainly in the mine planning division and rotated through 3 roles: shovel planning, roads planning, and structures planning.

As a shovel planning student, I managed the daily excavation plan for ore and waste across 9 electric and 2 hydraulic shovels. Through collaboration with engineers, geologists and operations leaders, I planned where the shovel would dig, the limits that it would dig up to, and precautions that should be taken to mitigate sloughing in the dig face. To understand dig limits, I analyzed geology and cross sections to classify the material as ore or waste as well as keeping sufficient width for the shovel to load trucks in double backup for optimal productivity. In areas where geology is challenging to manage unwanted clay content, I would design CAD surfaces on MicroStation and upload them to the shovel's system for the operator to follow. To manage geological discontinuities such as faults, I would guide the shovel on the direction that it would dig based on rose plots. I indicate operationally specific instructions for different conditions such as 'raking the face' when weather is cold making the material harder to mine, 'split facing' for hydraulic shovels in a dig face that is half-ore half-waste, and staying above a certain elevation level to avoid water or soft oil-sand rich ground that would risk the shovel getting stuck. I would say that shovel planning was the best place to start out for mine planning experience and provided a good base for, the subsequent roles I did.

I spent another 3 months in structures planning, monitoring material placement towards dumps and in-pit structures. I had become familiar already with waste locations and was able to collaborate with the shovels division and form plans to route the appropriate material for the construction. I expanded my geotechnical expertise by learning about the components of tailings dykes as well as the construction process and protocols. To monitor placement, I would track progress on Wenco, conduct daily field tours, cross reference the MicroStation design with Avenza Maps, and facilitate open communication between operators for feedback to optimally execute the task.

I spent the remainder of my term in roads planning. I planned and managed haul roads that provide the foundation for transportation between load and dump points. Similar to the previous roles, I conduct daily field tours to form plans for road maintenance, identifying the cause of uneven roads and allocating more grader effort on those areas or replacing a section with more competent material. Using my knowledge from shovels and structures planning, I was able to strategically plan routes that would be the most productive such as looking for straight shots across turns that didn't make sense, initiating the construction of ramps for shorter hauls, and keeping ahead of the shovel forecast to plan new routes replacing old ones that would be dug out. My most rewarding project I have done was designing and monitoring the construction of a semi-permanent limestone road from conception. I was mentored by an experienced operations leader and learned about the necesary equipment to do the job, material specifications that would pass as competent material, the geotechnical standards that must be met, and the procedures to be done in order to meet these standards. It was really rewarding for me as a Co-op student at the time to be responsible for such a large project and taught me a lot about project management, teamwork, and geotechnical design.

EDUCATION

University of British Columbia (Sep 2019 - May 2025)

I hold a Bachelors of Applied Science in Mining Engineering with a Minor in Data Science from the University of British Columbia.

The standard engineering program consists of completing a general engineering first year and then specializing in your intended engineering discipline (which for me was mining) in years 2 to 4. The standard completion time is 4 years but students can choose to extend their degree by a year (or more) to enroll in the Co-operative Education Program and complete work terms in between academics with various engineering companies that enhance the learning experience. During the last year, students complete a capstone project that aims to approach and recommend solutions for a real world problem presented by an engineering company.

In my program, I studied a breadth of facets in the mining industry such as Underground and Surface Mine Design, Engineering Geology, Rock Mechanics, Tailings Management, Mine Ventilation, Mining Equipment, Mineral Processing, and Engineering Economics. I gained additional quantitative exposure from my minor in data science in aspects such Machine Learning, Mathematical Optimization, Statistical Modelling and Relational Databases. I chose to do 2 years of co-op to gain experience in both open pit and underground mines, making me well rounded to work at different mines across the world. My capstone project was sponsored by Teck Resources Ltd.
